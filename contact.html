Reflections for DCD 110:Digital Storytelling

For the first reflection, I read chapters 1-3 of The Psychology of the Web Developer, Reality of a Female Freelancer by Maisa Imamovic. The first few chapters reflect on Imamovic's early encounters with language, migration, and the internet. She talks about how she used a software called Spleck to learn English, but she also encountered a humanistic relationship with the chatbot. After a while of using this, she started to realize the flaws of the software as it repeated meaningless questions to hers. She describes how that shaped her identity and understanding of digital spaces. In the next few chapters, titled Assistant Web Developer and The Web Developer, she discusses psychological pressures associated with web developing. From the perspectives of Larry and Scott, readers see Larry's intensive thought process behind building a website. These 2 chapters also highlight how seemingly simple websites have intense labor and complex systems behind them.

Chapters 4 and 5 really show how web development isn’t just about coding, but also about how people think and act in the tech world. In Chapter 4, it explains how “simple” websites are actually very complex behind the scenes, and how developers are pressured to keep up with constant changes and tools while also trying to make money. It made me realize that users don’t usually think about all the work behind a website, and developers don’t always have time to question what they’re creating. In Chapter 5, the idea of “patchwork culture” and Larry’s experience made it seem like constantly working with technology can make people feel disconnected from real life and even from themselves. Overall, these chapters show that being a web developer can be stressful and repetitive, and it raises questions about whether technology is actually helping people or just making everything feel more controlled and less personal.

Chapters 6, 7, and 8 continue to show the more personal and emotional side of working in tech, especially how it affects identity and mental health. These chapters focus a lot on the author’s experiences, like going through coding school and dealing with pressure to succeed quickly, which made it feel like everything was rushed and more about money than actual learning. The part about how users are expected to just click and accept everything without thinking really stood out, because it shows how people are kind of controlled by technology without realizing it. The last chapter, especially with the discussion of obsession and anorexia, connects how consuming too much information or media can actually impact someone’s real life and self-image in a negative way. Overall, these chapters made me think about how technology can shape who we are, not just what we do, and how it can sometimes lead to unhealthy habits or mindsets if we’re not careful.

Working with CSS for the first time was both interesting and frustrating because it was my first experience actually controlling how a website looks. At first, it seemed simple to change colors, fonts, and layouts, but it quickly got confusing when things didn’t show up the way I expected. One of the biggest challenges was understanding how different properties interact, like margins, padding, and positioning, since even small changes could completely mess up the layout. I also struggled with remembering the correct syntax and figuring out why my code wasn’t working, especially when it came to things like flexbox or aligning elements properly. Overall, learning CSS showed me that designing a website takes a lot of patience and problem-solving, but it was also rewarding to see my code turn into something visual on the screen.
